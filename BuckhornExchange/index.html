— A BRIEF HISTORY —

One hundred and
thirty‑one years of supper.

Four generations of the same family. One dining room. Fires that have not gone cold since Grover Cleveland was president.

1893
Shorty Scout opens the doors

Henry H. “Shorty Scout” Zietz — a young scout to Buffalo Bill Cody — opens The Buckhorn Exchange across from the railyards to feed cattle barons, silver kings and railroad men. The walls are already filling with his hunting trophies.

1900
Colorado Liquor License No. 1

The State of Colorado issues its very first liquor license. It is pressed into Shorty’s hand. That same license still hangs behind the bar, under glass, a century and a quarter later.

1905
A President at the table

Theodore Roosevelt stops in on his way to a bear hunt in Glenwood Springs. He is the first of five U.S. Presidents who will eventually dine under the buffalo head on the west wall.

1972
National Register of Historic Places

Eighty years after it opened, The Buckhorn Exchange is formally placed on the National Register of Historic Places — honoring not just the building, but an entire era of Denver still alive inside it.

Today
Still the same table

The fire still burns. The ribeye is still hand‑cut. The walls are still wild. And the first license Colorado ever issued still hangs above the last bar that ever really earned it.
